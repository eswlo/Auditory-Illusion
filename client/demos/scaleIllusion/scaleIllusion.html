Scale
Illusion

If you have analyzed the final movement of Tchaikovsky's 6th symphony, you may be intrigued by the first few measures amongst the strings: moving up and down in each part, these notes together sing a harmonized passage of descending scales. In terms of orchestration, such a technique produces a more dynamic flow between individual sections, both musically and acoustically. It is further enhanced by the common orchestra seating in the 19th century where the two violins sit on opposite sides, rendering a sort of panning effect.

Such a phenomenon can be explained by the Scale Illusion, which was noted and reported by Diana Deutsch back in 1973. Read the instructions and try the interactive demonstration by adjusting the two volume levels independently to explore the threshold where the illusion begins to merge. For further information, visit Deutsch's research webpage.
