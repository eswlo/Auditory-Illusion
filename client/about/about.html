CONCEPT


A fascinating topic within the broader field of auditory perception, auditory illusion has attracted the attention of researchers and scholars from a wide range of disciplines, such as psychology, neuroscience, and music. With a pedagogical goal in mind, this project was created to provide users with a more engaging means to experience, explore, and gain a more nuanced understanding of the topic. It was inspired in part by my graduate studies and teaching at Columbia, where my interaction with relevant materials resulted in the realization that most of the demonstrations available were pre-made. This observation eventually led to my plan of leveraging computing technology (in particular, Web Audio frameworks) and programming to model and synthesize these phenomena in real-time, hoping to facilitate students' learning, instructors' teaching, other educational aspects, etc.

Currently, there are three types of auditory illusion included in this project: stream segregation (based on spectral peak position), scale illusion, and octave illusion. The first one was drawn from Al Bregman's experiments, while the other two came from Diana Deutsch's research on illusions. More selected demonstrations will be implemented continuously.

Special thanks to Prof. Brad Garton for sparking my interest in the application of Web Audio frameworks, and to Prof. Fred Lerdahl for guiding me to explore the intersection of cognition and sound.

Despite his extensive training in music, Lo has maintained a great level of curiosity toward science and computing technology. After obtaining his Doctor of Musical Arts (DMA) at Columbia and working as a freelance composer for a few years, Lo decided to return to school in order to develope a strong foundation and competency in computing and software engineering. He is currently pursuing a degree in computer science at the University of British Columbia in Vancouver, Canada.
